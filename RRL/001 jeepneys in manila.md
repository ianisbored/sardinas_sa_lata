# Origins
## Gotangco et al., 2025

## Adovas et al., 2024

Not only does it serve as a mode of transportation, but it also provides a livelihood that enables opportunity to flow among Filipino people of low socio-economic status in order to sustain their daily necessities in life.
## Quilantang, 2024
- The jeepney, a distinctive form of transportation unique
- to the Philippines, is rooted in the aftermath of World War 2 when the Americans left remnants of their equipment.
- Originally named as Willy’s Jeep, it was designed as a lightweight military vehicle to navigate rugged terrain and carry troops and supplies; resourceful Filipinos repurposed and customised them to adapt to the country’s post-war needs. These modified vehicles were imbued with local aesthetics and decorated with ornate and vivid designs, transforming them into mobile canvases for Filipino folk art, symbols, and inscriptions that reflected the country’s cultural identity, aspirations, and creative spirit. 
- Drawing aesthetic inspiration from the casco (a covered riverboat variant popular during the Spanish era) and the calesa (an intricately designed horse carriage that still exists today), the jeepney blends history, functionality, and artistic expression into a uniquely Filipino narrative
## Parrucho, 2023
- The United States military left a surplus of military jeeps in the Philippines at the end of the war. These jeeps were originally intended for military use, transporting troops during the war. However, after the military left, the jeeps were repurposed and transformed intopublic utility vehicles to address the Filipino people's post-war transportation needs.

## Blanton, 2015
- In terms of its historical background, the Jeepney represents a creative re-assemblage of components of the American war machine. More specifically, thousands of all-terrain vehicles know as “jeeps” were abandoned as surplus throughout the Philippines after the Second World War. With remarkable ingenuity, these surplus vehicles were modified with an elongated bed and new roof in order to provided much-needed public transportation  vehicles for a country whose infrastructure had been decimated by the war. Yet what was only meant to be a temporary fix to the infrastructural woes of the Philippines not only persisted throughout the second half of the twentieth century, but the general form of these early Jeepneys still persists as the most popular form of public transportation throughout the Philippines.

## Blanton, 2015
- The Jeepney is a small bus carrying around 20 passengers seated facing one another  on two benches running parallel to the length of the vehicle. In the urban transportation literature, the Jeepney has been categorized as a form of paratransit. This term  emerged in the early 1970s to describe unconventional forms of transportation, that,  opposed to city buses and commuter trains, operated outside the conventional fixed route genre. *Kirby et al., Paratransit; Cervero, Informal Transport; Rimmer, Role of Paratransit; Rimmer, Rikisha to Rapid Transit.* At its most basic level, paratransit describes creative forms of mobility  that emerge when large bureaucracies and their concomitant infrastructures of transportation fail to meet the demands of the commuting public. With the increasing  inefficiency of state sanctioned systems of bus and light rail, the Jeepney has flourished to become a ubiquitous presence on the extremely congested streets of Manila.
##  Güss & Tuason, 2008
- Jeepneys are very colorful. Many jeepneys has  bright colors to attract attention in the crowded streets. The most common decorations included additional lights and sound accessories such as sound systems followed by religious and aesthetic items. such as figurines, toys, curtains. Among the functional decorations were small fans, sampaguita flowers, air fresheners, lights, small mirrors, and repurposed trash cans.
- Temperatures often reach 35 degrees Celsius and passengers are squeezed together in the limited space. Extra lights and mirrors in the front allow the driver to observe what passengers are doing in the back—specifically, if commuters leave without paying.
- Sometimes a driver hires someone to keep track of the passengers and collect fares. The jeepney driver is an example of multitasking. While driving a manual transmission jeep in traffic, during a heat wave or a typhoon, he has bills between his fingers and coins in a small can, he stops to let people off and on, collects fares and gives exact change, and deals with occasional quarrels between passengers or with other drivers. Due to the nature of the occupation, drivers have decorations to provide some sense of security and comfort.

## Quilantang, 2024
- The jeepney, a widely utilised mode of public transportation, not only serves as a means of transport but also embodies Filipino culture, identity, and social stories. It is essential to recognise that vehicles, such as jeepneys, do not merely serve as neutral modes of transportation, but rather play an active role in shaping and being shaped by society.
- Today, the jeepney symbolises Filipino ingenuity, innovation, and resilience, and is no longer just a modified version of the American military jeep.
- The concept of mobility goes beyond just physical movement. It also includes the circulation of symbols, ideas, and cultural narratives. Jeepneys in the Philippines are not just a means of transportation, but they also act as vehicles of cultural mobility. They are moving canvases that display and transport diverse cultural symbols throughout the urban landscape.
- Jeepney decorations are a diverse and colourful representation of Filipino culture, values, and aspirations. These decorations convey a vibrant narrative of Filipino life and experiences through a combination of various motifs. 
- Still, they also communicate complex cultural messages while on the move. Through the intricate fusion of traditional, contemporary, and global influences visible on these mobile canvases.
- Additionally, the artistry and craftsmanship of decorating jeepneys is truly remarkable. Both jeepney owners and artists take great pride in their work, creating intricate designs that artfully depict historical events, religious icons, landscapes, and local traditions. These designs serve as a means of artistic expression and storytelling, showcasing the Philippines’ incredible creativity and cultural heritage. From these perspectives, the jeepney is not just a symbol but an agent of culture, embodying and transporting cultural narratives across the nation.
- The phase-out of traditional jeepneys may disrupt the cultural exchanges, social interactions, and economic activities that unfold within these vehicles. Moreover, the proposed standardised designs of modern jeepneys may create uniform and monotonous public spaces, thereby impacting the aesthetic experiences of city dwellers and commuters.

## Biona, 2017
- The Philippine public utility jeepney (PUJ) is an iconic mode of transportation that symbolizes the ingenuity, and capacity to adapt, of the Filipino people.
- Jeepneys meet a significant proportion of the public transport demand in the country. For example, they account for around 74 million passenger kilometers travelled in Metro Manila annually (*Department of Transportation and Communications, 2011, Development of a Mega Manila Public Transportation Planning Support System (MMPTPSS), Project Report. Mega Manila Public Transport Study.*) and carry about one-half of all peak-period passenger trips in the metropolis.
- It could be noted that the top three types of modal transfers in Metro Manila are between jeepneys and tricycles, jeepneys and jeepneys, and jeepney and buses (*Department of Transportation and Communication, 1999, Metro Manila Urban Transportation Integration Study, Public Transport, Technical Report No. 9.*).
- The dominance of these vehicles could be attributed to a number of factors, including:
	- Historical land use planning and development generated a relatively dispersed travel demand that is not dense enough to be served by larger public transport modes such as buses.
	- In existing jeepney routes where larger public transport vehicles operate, shifting towards buses is difficult due to political and social factors. 
	- Jeepneys have lower fares compared to buses while providing generally higher levels of service.
	- Jeepneys provide unrestricted pick-up and drop-off points along their routes. 
	- Manufacturing technology and capacity is locally available, and jeepney parts such as second-hand engines and imported chassis are readily available.
	- Jeepney operations are a major source of jobs and income to a large range of society, many of whom are less suited for other types of employment due to their limited qualifications.
	- The jeepneys have intermediate sizes and capacities that make them operable under the configuration of the country’s road network, enabling jeepneys to easily move, stop, load and unload passengers as well as penetrate even the smallest interior areas.
- A prior study done by the Manila Observatory (*Subida, R., Velas, M, Mcnamara, D. (2004). Integrated Environmental Strategies –Philippines Project Report/Metropolitan Manila: A Focus on the Transport Sector.*) stated that diesel-fed jeepneys contribute 15% of the particulate matter emissions in Metro Manila. Although the jeepneys are not explicitly separated as a category, they form majority of the UV (utility vehicle) diesel vehicles, which are the most important category in terms of particulate matter (48%). These vehicles also significantly contribute to SOx (27%) and NOx (21%) emissions.
