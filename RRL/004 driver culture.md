# Values
## Güss & Tuason, 2008
- Filipino values can be characterized as high collectivism (preference for acting as group members rather than as individuals), very high power distance (degree of acceptance of inequality), low uncertainty avoidance (preference for unstructured rather than structured situations), high masculinity (preference for values such as assertiveness and performance over those of warm personal relationships and service), and high short-term orientation (opposite of a long-term time orientation that stresses Confucian values like persistence).
## Javidan & House, 2001; Smith et al., 2002; Inglehart and Baker, 2000; Enriquez, 1977; Paredes, 1996, as cited in Güss & Tuason, 2008
- Javidan & House (2001) characterized the Philippines as very high on humane orientation, meaning that Filipinos try to avoid conflict in conversations and are caring and supportive.\
- Smith et al. (2002) combined data from several value surveys and characterized Filipinos as high in power distance and conservatism, i.e., showing preference for the immediate circle over outsiders, maintaining the status quo, and loyalty towards one’s boss.
- Similarly, Inglehart and Baker (2000), in a study of 65 countries, characterized individuals from the Philippines as having high traditional (religion, national pride, and obedience to authority) and survival (economic and physical security) values 
- For Enriquez, the Filipino self is not an independent unity, but an interconnected unity with the selves of others or with the kapwa (the other). Thus, the Western concept of collectivism only captures a small aspect of kapwa. Among the more frequently mentioned values according to Enriquez (1977) were pakikisama (favoring smooth interpersonal relations), utang na loob (honoring debt of gratitude), and bahala na (determination in the face of uncertainty).
- Paredes (1996) mentioned utang kabubut-ón (debt of gratitude), pakikisama (camaraderie), and hiya (shame).

# Lamangan (one-upmanship)
##  Menez, 1998
- Refers to the strategy for besting one’s opponent in any kind of contest. However, to the jeepney driver, it is a game which governs not only his driving behavior but also his pursuit of the good life.
- Driving is also an economic competition, for the driver’s daily income depends upon the number of passengers he can transport to their destinations. He weaves in and out of traffic, his eyes alternately on the road, on the look-out for potential customers, and on his passengers getting on or off his vehicle; with one hand on the wheel, the other collecting fares and handing out change, and intermittently wiping off his sweat with a rag tied around his neck, the jeepney driver is indeed the highly pressured urbanite in the marketplace of city streets. As one driver wrote on his jeep, “Jingle” lang ang pahinga [We rest only when we piss].
- The use of elegant titles is another manifestation of the competition for social status. Such jeepney names as “Jeepney King II,” “Road Emperor,” “Jason the King,” “Prince William,” and “Denis D’Great” state the drivers’ playful attempts to affiliate with royalty. If one is a “Millionaire Forever,” the other is a “Socialite Forever.” Even such signs as Solong Anak [Only Child] and Heredero [Heir] express some form of social one-upmanship. They refer to the special treatment accorded an only child or a sole heir who never had to do without the basic necessities of life. Laki sa Layaw: Jeeprox (Raised in Luxury: Jeeprox] is a variant of the refrain from a popular song “Jeprocks” [Projects] about the self-indulgent children of suburbia. Like the elegant titles above, these allusions to a privileged childhood are the driver’s fantasy. 
- Jeepney drivers, however, have a different interpretation. One gave this typical explanation: “A jeepney is just like a woman who loves beautiful clothes. If we do well we dress her up.” This analogy is also found in such inscriptions as “A jeepney without a driver is like a woman without a lover,” and its recent variant, “A chick without a lover is like a Mazda without a stereo.”
- This intimate identification with the vehicle as an extension of the driver’s personality emphasizes the jeepney’s function as a status symbol. As another driver pointed out, “If the driver is prosperous, so is his jeep.” The quantity and quality of decorative objects provide a gauge of the driver’s economic status. A jeep with six or eight chrome statuettes of Pegasus is deemed superior to one with only two or three chrome horses. One-upmanship is the name of the game.
## Stone, 1973, as cited in Menez, 1998
- To the jeepney driver, one-upmanship pertains primarily to the acquisition of road space. Driving is a constant competition for space, with each driver bent on edging out his competitors to avoid being obstructed

Religion is also expressed in the actions. Often, drivers mumble a short prayer at the start of their work day or when beginning a route while passengers make the sign of a cross when passing a church or passing an accident.

## Blanton, 2015
- The Jeepney is not only the dominant mode of public transportation in Manila, but a crucial representational vehicle in the religious life of the city. Since at least the  early 1950s, the exterior of the Jeepney has been an important site of Filipino folk  art, featuring symbols of speed and masculinity such as abstract representations of  rooster wings creatively “split” around the exterior panels of the vehicle folk art coincided with new charismatic Christian and evangelical religious movements that packed large coliseums and enlivened new communities of collective effervescence in Manila in the mid 1980s. Directly coincident with these new religious  movements, the metallic surface of the Jeepney became filled with brightly colored  spray paint lettering proclaiming pious slogans such as “Praise the Lord!” “Prayer  Warrior” and “Power of Prayer”. Likewise, these movements signaled  an increasing orientation of the Jeepneys’ exterior space of visual representation  around the themes of the Virgin Mary [Roman Catholic], biblical characters, and prayer [predominantly evangelical and charismatic Christian]. 
- These mobile Marian apparitions reflect the shrines located just to the side of  the road in residential neighborhoods throughout Manila. Many of these shrines display a ceramic statue of Mary adorned with silk flowers and surrounded by the residual traces of wax from the burning of votive candles. From her  niche of cobblestone and mortar, Mary casts a plangent gaze toward the crossroads.
- At night her shrine is anointed by the sallow luminescence of a soot-stained light  bulb: a sacred beacon on streets prone to accident, contingency and breakdown.  
- As vehicle of religious representation, the Jeepney marks the proliferation of pious  visual culture within urban public space. Through the mobile surfaces of the Jeepney,  the presence of pious imagery has transitioned from the private devotional shrine  of the domestic interior and the candlelit space of the Cathedral and, quite literally,  taken to the street. As an apparatus of mobile piety, the Jeepney takes the annual Festival procession of the Saints and plunges this pious imagery into the vibrant  circulation of everyday life.
- This urban transit research describes how these new forms of pious imagery are  not merely a passive reflection of religious movements occurring around them in the  spaces of churches, cathedrals and public coliseums; rather, these vibrant images themselves actively refashion the urban landscape into an enchanted mobile network  of miraculous Marian apparitions, fervent communal prayer, and pious exhortations.  In this way, the infrastructure of public transportation becomes an apparatus of  urban belief, mapping the grid and the informal economies of everyday life that take  place upon its asphalt surface with sacred visions shrouded in delicate plumes of dust  and diesel fumes.
- In terms of infrastructure and materiality, the Jeepney is not only a ‘vehicle’ of  representation, but the form of the technology itself is ‘quickened’ or actuated by the  force of religious community and the experience of sacred presence. For an example  of the way religion is fabricated into the very materiality of the infrastructure, we  could take the flying dove, a symbol of the immanent power of the Holy Spirit in the  Christian tradition – descending through the polished steel surface of a Jeepney side panel
- In this instance, religious representation and instrumental function become indistinguishable within the shimmering form of “stainless” steel. Thus the idea of divine communication and the miraculous traffic between the sacred and the everyday has  become so sedimented within the history of the Jeepney that it is now “fabricated”  into the structure of the machine in the independent manufacturing and assembly  garages located around the city. This “stainless” steel suggests a promising method to  describe and theorize urban infrastructure in many developing countries, not merely  as an assemblage of bureaucratic practices and technical instrumentalities whose  functions can be calculated, consciously managed and rationally controlled, but as a  machine ensemble whose “functioning” is undergirded by an excessive underbelly of  miraculous appearances, prayers, and prestige.
- Guarding over this coin box is a reliquary of devotional objects: rosaries swaying to  the rhythmic ensemble of combustion engines and traffic lights, a small statue of the  Santo Niño in his gesture of divine wisdom, perfumed garlands of freshly-blessed  flowers, printed images of the saints, and small woven curtains featuring pious  phrases such as “God is Love.” As previously mentioned, the communal practice of  fare collection culminates at the base of this shrine, an offering that not only ensures  that one maintains a legitimate space within the Jeepney, but a metaphysical insurance of safe passage on urban streets prone to accident, breakdown and contingency

# Love and Sex
## Menez, 1998
- The most important area of competition is sex. A common stereotype of the jeepney driver, well accepted by themselves, is that of the great lover, “Lady Killer,” “Chick Hunter,”, “I am the greatest.” The image of the great lover is also associated with economic success as a driver. 
- However, [[#^1f1c99|all the three cautionary messages]] also allude to the hazards involved in extramarital affairs. A kabit [clinger] in Tagalog slang is a mistress; a singit [wedge] is the third party in a love triangle. 
- Extramarital affairs are a favorite topic for jeepney commentary. A common term for cheating on one’s spouse or lover is the Tagalog slang kumakaliwa [literally, making a left turn]. Everyone knows therefore, that the sign Huwag kaliwa on a jeepney does not literally mean “Do not make a left turn.”
- Another word for unfaithful is kalewete [literally, left-handed], which appears in a driver’s prayer, Dios ko, iligtas mo po ako sa babaeng kalewete [My God, deliver me from a “left-handed” woman]. 
- Once more, the concept of lamangan [one upmanship] is applicable here since, as mentioned previously, the number of mistresses a driver can afford to keep is supposedly a gauge of his social status. Furthermore, a great lover should also be able to “cut in” or take away someone’s wife or lover. In a society with a double standard of sexual morality, it is usually the man who gets away with extramarital affairs. Theoretically, however, in cultural terms it is the “left-handed” wife of a “left-turning” husband who should ultimately win the game of one-upmanship. 
- A number of these messages are reminders to pay one’s fare, “Pay as you enter,” “Forget everything but not your fare.” Some are warnings about occupying more space than warranted, like the caption on a sticker showing a half naked lady with her legs crossed, Kaunting ipit, baka po masilip ng mga matang mainit [Cross your legsa little tighter, these “hot” eyes might take a peek].

Its colourful, rugged exterior, the roar of its engine, and the thrill of manoeuvring through dense city traffic all contribute to the image of the jeepney as a masculine entity.
- The masculine connotation is further underscored by the fact that jeepney driving, a profession predominantly occupied by men, requires physical strength, courage, and assertiveness, characteristics stereotypically associated with masculinity.

As these images travel through urban and rural areas, they engage with the public, actively disseminating and reinforcing societal norms around beauty, romance, desire, and gender roles. The mobility of these images amplifies their impact as they become a consistent and widespread aspect of the visual landscape, contributing to the public’s collective understanding and reinforcement of these norms.

# Politics
## Güss & Tuason, 2008
- Jeepneys are also verbose in politics expressed in decorations such as ‘No to oil price hike’ and political campaign ads. 
- Jeepney drivers, especially, are extensively aware of politics as they understand the impact it has on their work. Out of nationalism or desperation, Filipinos are highly involved in movements against governmental institutions running the country. For instance, several non-violent manifestations successfully toppled a dictator in 1986. 
- These movements were attended by millions of Filipinos who prayed, held vigils, sang, and stayed in the streets until the incumbent president fled or stepped down. People Power movements attest to the Filipino’s pakikibaka (advocacy for change or some kind of rebellion) and pakikisama sa kapwa (cooperation with others). 
- This political awareness is still relevant in modern times. For instance, the government's modernization program in 2022 continuing today, which sought to phase out traditional jeepneys for standardized electric vehicles, was received by many drivers as a cultural and political erasure, the removal of one of the most prominent symbols of the working-class

# Family
## Bastos, 2008
- Filipinos’ notion of a self is more attached to the family than to the individual. The authors stress the insufficiency of the Philippines’ system of social protection, where the family is the main or the only source of security. 
## Güss & Tuason, 2008)
- Names written on jeepneys are also a common sight, including names of drivers, their loved ones—spouses, parents, or children—coined names for the jeepney, or fabricated names that represent two or three people’s names combined (e.g., KhitJanLy, Pol-pog part 1). 
- These names express honor and affection towards people significant in drivers’ lives. Names on jeepneys show a preference towards people (makatao) and the value of family relationships. They signify perseverance (pagsisikap) for someone or gratitude (utang na loob).
## Quilantang, 2024
- Family plays a crucial role in Filipino culture and is often symbolically represented on jeepneys (Flores). Symbols such as family portraits, names, and depictions of familyoriented activities frequently adorn jeepneys. These icons bear a direct meaning linked to the concept of family and kinship. 
- However, these symbols also embody Filipinos’ deep value on family relationships and the role of the family as a source of motivation and strength.
- In the context of the jeepney driver, the symbols often represent their connection to their loved ones, reminding them of whom they work hard for and why they persist in a challenging occupation.
- For passengers, these symbols elicit a sense of empathy and connection, acknowledging that the driver is not just a service provider but a person with a family relying on them. This understanding can positively influence the interactions between drivers and passengers, promoting a sense of community and shared human experience. Additionally, the prominence of family symbols on jeepneys can reflect the collective Filipino identity, which highly values familial ties and obligations.
# Driving Speed

## Menez, 1998
- Juxtaposed with this portrayal of the relentless struggle for economic survival is the ostentatious, uninhibited style of jeepney folk art. According to Emmanuel Torres, the excessive embellishments are due to horror vacui, “a fear of empty space, that seemingly compulsive filling up of every square inch of surface commonly observed in popular visual arts,” resulting in modern rococo decor. 
- The assemblage of assorted objects representing speed is original jeepney art: chrome miniature wings, missiles and winged horses on the hood, paintings of birds and airplanes on the sides 
- Such “special effects” are intended to attract the commuter and to impress upon the public that the driver is not only economically successful but also au courant. Paintings of rural scenes are often commissioned by the owners.
## Quilantang, 2024
- Speed motifs on jeepneys, such as chrome miniature wings, missiles, birds, and aeroplanes, offer a fascinating entry point for understanding Filipino culture and society. 
- These symbols directly refer to speed, power, and motion—elements inherently associated with the vehicle itself and its function as a mode of transport. The wings, for instance, denote the concept of flight, representing high speed and freedom (Torres 14-18). Similarly, missiles signify swiftness and power, while birds and airplanes serve as universal emblems of fast travel.
- Moreover, the emphasis on speed also mirrors broader societal dynamics. In the fast-paced urban setting where jeepneys operate, speed may connote efficiency, competitiveness, and a relentless drive to keep up with the demands of daily life.

# Language
## Menez, 1998
- Linguistic play is a prominent feature in Philippine languages, and by extension, its jeepney inscriptions, enriched by the multilingual resources of the city. Jeepney inscriptions use the three dominant languages in the nation’s capital—Tagalog (on which the national language, Pilipino, is based), English (which is still considered an official language), and Taglish (a mixture of the two languages). Typically “mix-mix,” as Taglish is also known, is the sign, “No parking” sa hindi mo “darling” [Don’t park with someone whois not your darling]. Literal translations of Tagalog slang into English like the sign, “Left—turning Lover” [unfaithful lover] *Jeepney drivers speak Tagalog but typically have learned some English in elementary school and from the media.*
- Their favorite form of verbal manipulation is the transposition of syllables, Erfat [Father], or letters, Jeprocks, Jeeprox, Jeffrox, etc., for “Projects” [Jeprocts]. Punning is also used with words or sounds from Tagalog, English, and Spanish, as in “God knows Hudas not pay.” [Tagalog/Spanish, pronounced “who does” for the Biblical traitor Judas Iscariot]. 
- The superimposition of native and foreign words on one another has produced such signs as “tSuperman”  [tsuper: driver] and MACHOnurin [Macho + masunurin, obedient].
- The content and style of these linguistic, as well as artistic, innovations reflect the flamboyance, earthy humor, satiric wit, and daredevil driving style of their creators. These “kings of the road” live and work according to their philosophy, “Work hard, enjoy hard,” which underlies their competitive strategies for achieving power and status. 
- Jeepney messages often offer advice couched in rhymes with double meanings like the following examples seen on three different vehicles:  
	- Basta kabit, may sabit [If you hang on, you'll get caught (snagged)] 
	- Puede kabit, puera sabit [You may hang on, but don’t get caught] 
	- Puede singit, huwag ipit [You may cut in, but don’t get caught in between] ^1f1c99
- The first two slogans ostensibly warn passengers about the danger of hanging on to the handle in the rear entrance of the jeepney while trying to board the vehicle. The third slogan is addressed to other drivers about the risks of cutting in.

# Inscriptions
## Menez, 1998
- Jeepney inscriptions are rhetorical devices for attaining social and material gains. They are addressed not only to fellow motorists but also to everyone else on the street, especially prospective passengers. Just as the rich ornamentation is intended to attract the more discerning commuters, these messages are meant to entice the potential rider, thus, Kung nais mo ay ligaya, sa akin ka sumakay/sumama [If happiness is your desire, ride with me (variant: go out with me)], and to provide entertainment for the duration of the ride. The driver in turn has an appreciative audience

> When you ride the Philippine jeepney, you don’t read a newspaper. You read the jeepney itself. For a jeepney is graffiti; it is the driver’s advertisement for himself. All those prayers, mottos, proverbs, captions and one-liners that clutter up the dashboard are meant to communicate this fact: that the driver with his back to you is a man, he is alive, and like his vehicle, he is unique. *Nestor Torre, “The Jeepney as Literature,” Archipelago 1:4 (1974): 6.*

- [[#^ad1602|These messages]] are instead intended to relieve some of the tensions generated by the frenetic pace of urban life: the competition for passengers on the part of the driver, the competition for a ride on the part of the commuter, and for all who work and live on city streets, environmental pressures such as the extreme tropical heat, the fumes from the vehicles, and the hazards and aggravations of frequently flooded streets. 
- As city dwellers—drivers and passengers—battle with the impossible traffic in Manila’s congested streets, jeepney folk art and inscriptions provide a relief from what would otherwise be a grim battlefield. As Alan Dundes and Carl Pragter point out: “While urban life may produce alienation, it also generates urban folklore to help make the ills and pressures of modern society just a little bit more bearable.” (Dundes & Pagter, 1976)

According to Adovas et al. (2024), most of the jeepney drivers are still struggling financially to provide for their daily necessities despite being the primary source of transportation for a lot of Filipinos. Many drivers pay for their jeepney fees, and the remaining budget is for their food and family needs. 

Additionally, as mentioned in the study of Cunanan et al. (2024), jeepney drivers typically earned around ₱500 per day, while jeepney operators normally earn between ₱2,500 and ₱3,000. The high cost of modern jeepney exceeds the capacity on operators and drivers, placing a heavy financial burden on both of them. As a result, drivers are forced to work long hours just to keep up with expenses. 

Rodriguez (2024) explained that in the modernization of jeepneys, the concerns of jeepney drivers are their salaries. They will no longer be paid a regular salary regardless of the number of passengers they serve each day, and they will need to abandon the "boundary system" they used to. Modernization can help lessen the careless driving and accidents on the road, however, it can result in high costs for drivers and operators which also affects the fares of commuters.

In the study of Kecorius et al. (2017), in the Philippines, particularly in Manila, ultrafine soot particles or black carbon (BC) are present in the environment and it causes adverse respiratory and cardiovascular effects which increases the cases of asthma and premature deaths. This affects jeepney drivers and commuters the most, as they are exposed to polluted air daily.

Furthermore, Seva et al. (2011), found that it is not only the passengers who can experience discomfort while riding a jeepney but also the jeepney drivers that were likely to endure discomfort, due to poor design of their workspace. Their current workspace did not go along with the body measurements of the drivers  and the drivers were subjected to awkward posture, due to the location of controls and task of collecting fares. 

Beltran et al. (2024) emphasized that long working hours, low income, traffic congestion, challenging job environments, and road construction contribute to increased dangerous driving among jeepney drivers. Similarly, Cañada et al. (2021) stated that physical or mental exhaustion that resulted in fatigue can reduce attentiveness, poor judgment, and slower reaction times, which significantly increases risky driving practices. These challenges faced by the drivers are important to consider because they can affect passengers safety and comfort during daily commutes.
