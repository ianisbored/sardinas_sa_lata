# Definiton
## Cabilin, 2025
- The jeepney is a part of the lives of us Filipinos. A passenger may use it their entire life as transportation for going to and coming home from work or school.
- In the jeepney, the Filipino trait of *pakikipagkapwa* (relating to others) is prominent, which is considered the core or most important value among Filipinos from the perspective of Filipino Psychology (*Sikolohiyang Pilipino*). This field was pioneered by Virgilio Enriquez in the early 1970s to advance the study of psychology rooted in Filipino experience, thought, and orientation, amid a landscape dominated by Western perspectives in the country (Enriquez, 2015).
- Therefore, the core of this field is the enrichment of the Filipino psyche with an emphasis on the concept of *kapwa* (shared identity/fellow being). *Kapwa* is the unity of "self" and "other," as it recognizes a shared identity.
- The concept of _kapwa_ is not merely a personal matter; it also involves connection and mutual empathy. According to Dagmang (1996), when a Filipino wants to say something, they express it through feelings; when they want to know something, they sense it (_nakikiramdam_). In philosophical discourse on _kapwa_, it is viewed as relational and inseparable from a deeper understanding of the human person (Mabaquiao, 2018).
- If there is one element to be considered the most important in the jeepney, it is none other than the person—its passenger. From the passenger stems the very reason why the jeepney was created.
- It can be observed that the passenger is a subject who knows their destination and the reason why they boarded the jeepney. They have a direction and purpose that they form while observing the events inside and outside the jeepney. Part of their direction is to demonstrate ethics toward fellow passengers and the driver, such as passing fare payments, politely using the word _"po"_ (a term of respect), and courteously signaling to stop and get off. This is a clear demonstration of _pakikipagkapwa-tao_ (relating to others as fellow human beings).
- Understanding is an essential ingredient in maintaining the order and relationships formed among each participant in the jeepney. This stems from the natural tendency of Filipinos toward _pakikipagkapwa-tao_ with others, whether they are strangers or not.
- One proof of this is the act of scooting over in one's seat to make space for others to sit. Additionally, the driver lowers the volume of his radio whenever he sees or hears a passenger seriously talking on the phone.
- In such examples, it is clearly seen how understanding is natural and does not often need to be reminded before it is shown and felt. In the jeepney, Filipino values can be illustrated through an understanding of the concept of _pakikipagkapwa-tao_.
- According to Timbreza, from an interview by Villejo and Liwanag (2019): "Through reflection on the negative behaviors of Filipinos, we also come to understand the Filipino character. We are able to bring out and make understood the various behaviors of Filipinos, whether positive or negative—the personhood of the people is opened up. Weaknesses and shortcomings are shown not to slander others, but to help understand these weaknesses and shortcomings as part of being human, and to strengthen and develop the positive and good qualities that need to be reinforced." This point was valued in the study in order to fully express both the positive and negative behaviors of passengers inside the jeepney, which further highlighted the formation of _kapwa_ among fellow jeepney passengers.
- According to Matienzo (2021), this experience reflects social relationships, the value of humanity, and lessons that are unique only to this mode of transportation. The jeepney is a vehicle through which one can personally witness social connections—not only between a driver and his fellow drivers, or between the driver and the passenger, but especially between a passenger and his fellow passengers. People are the very center of this study because, whether verbal or non-verbal, one can trace in a person the concrete world they feel in relation to their fellow human beings.

# +"Bayad po"
### Cabilin, 2025
- Common among the recorded data provided by the adolescents (_lalabintaunin_) is the act of passing fare payments to the driver from any fellow passenger in the jeepney. They called this a "normal" practice of Filipinos in the jeepney because it seems like whoever is closest to the driver has no choice but to pass the fare. There is no sign posted nor anyone ordering them to pass the fare, yet this is innate to Filipino behavior. In the act of paying—holding the money—it is already a sign and understood by fellow passengers what it is for and what to do with what is being handed over, accompanied by phrases like _"makikisuyo"_ or _"pakisuyo"_ (may I ask a favor), _"po"_ (a term of respect), and _"salamat po"_ (thank you, with respect) as signs of courtesy.
- Receiving or accepting fare from a fellow passenger is a sign of their respect, whether old or young. A connection is formed between the one paying and the one receiving, even when there are times the person being handed the fare is doing something else—such as talking to someone, looking in another direction, having their eyes closed, or listening to music. The act of passing fare in the jeepney is considered an "unspoken language"—a language that no longer needs to be spoken—according to one adolescent respondent.
- The honesty of each fellow passenger in the jeepney was also observed, especially regarding the amount to be paid and the change to be returned. According to the adolescents interviewed, they have not yet had an experience of someone not paying honestly or receiving insufficient change. There are two movements of fare money in the jeepney: first, from the passenger who does not always have the exact fare, has no coins, or carries only bills—even when there is a large sign that reads _"barya lang po sa umaga"_ (coins only in the morning, please); second, the passenger's change from the driver. In both movements, these are passed along by fellow passengers, especially when many are on board. In the end, their fare is correct and their change is received. This kind of practice demonstrates the honesty of each fellow passenger in the jeepney. Though what is felt in the hand when passing fare may be mere coins, it still proves the innate goodness and honesty of Filipinos, especially toward their fellow human beings.

# +"Pára po sa Tabi" (Cabilin, 2025)
- Similar to the passing of fares, which uses both verbal and physical means, another distinctly Filipino characteristic of _pakikipagkapwa_ in the jeepney is the act of signaling a stop whenever a passenger is boarding or leaving.
- The word _pára_ refers to the stopping of a moving vehicle such as a jeepney. When a passenger says _pára_, the driver pulls the jeepney to the side to let passengers board or alight. The driver immediately understands the signal whenever _pára_ is said loudly and clearly.
- As for the non-verbal _pára_, it was observed among teenagers in the form of tapping the roof of the jeepney to produce a sound. This sound serves as a signal to the driver that a passenger wishes to alight. This is done using a finger or a coin knocked against the metal handlebars to produce a signal sound. Another non-verbal method is pulling a cord, which simultaneously lights up a bulb facing the driver. Despite these alternatives, verbally saying _"pára po sa tabi"_ and producing a signal sound remain the most commonly used methods.
- The interactions among passengers in the jeepney reflect two distinctly Filipino characteristics. The concept of _kapwa_ is specifically expressed through the act of passing fares and change, as well as assisting with stop signals. There are moments when a passenger acts independently, yet fellow passengers inevitably step in to help. This act of assistance reflects the _pakikisalamuha_ of one passenger toward another.

# +Tinginan at Pakiramdaman (Cabilin, 2025)
- Among the teenage males interviewed, they all shared a tendency to become observant of their surroundings inside the jeepney. They would look at the people already seated or about to board, checking whether they were acquaintances or strangers.
- It is a common scene to see passengers sleeping in the jeepney at night, especially during rush hour or when office workers and students are heading home. Sleeping or dozing off is a sign of their exhaustion from a full day of work or study. In these situations, many fellow passengers become noticeably considerate, as observed by the teenage respondents. This is expressed through minimal movement, lowering their voices when talking, and waking a fellow passenger who has fallen deeply asleep but needs to alight. There are also those who unavoidably lean on the person beside them while sleeping, and fellow passengers simply let it be.
- For passengers carrying many bags, those with children, the elderly, or persons with disabilities (PWD), other passengers immediately make space and offer assistance. These passengers are typically seated at the front near the entrance or close to the driver. None of the teenage respondents had ever encountered a situation where a fellow passenger did not offer help to someone in this condition. The respondents also observed the driver's attentiveness to what was happening inside the jeepney. For instance, when a passenger was engaged in conversation, the driver would immediately lower the volume of the radio as a sign of consideration and awareness of the situation.
- Similar to the distinctly Filipino practices of fare payment and signaling a stop, the levels of social interaction — _pakikitungo_, _pakikisalamuha_, and _pakikilahok_ — are observable inside the jeepney. These levels of interaction are typically preceded by _tinginan_ (mutual looking) and _pakiramdaman_ (feeling out the situation) before a deeper form of engagement is reached. The distinctly Filipino characteristics of _tinginan_ and _pakiramdaman_ can therefore be considered the first stage through which these three levels of social interaction are initiated and carried out.

# +Kuwentuhan (Cabilin, 2025)
- Every act of _tinginan_ and _pakiramdaman_ among passengers in the jeepney prompts action to connect with fellow passengers, such as through _kuwentuhan_ or conversation. Storytelling and conversation come naturally to Filipinos, as we are inclined toward involvement and engagement with others.
- The teenage respondents noted that the jeepney serves as a space where _kuwentuhan_ begins.
- The example shared by the respondent illustrates how one person's experience can become the experience of others as well. It becomes an opportunity for collective agreement or possible disagreement, which then drives the direction of the conversation. The exchange only came to a stop when the elderly passenger alighted from the jeepney. That brief conversation, however, had already traversed a long conceptual journey in building _kapwa_ among fellow passengers. Its quality of _ibang-tao_ was reflected in the act of _pakikibagay_ — adjusting oneself to the experience being lived by the older passenger. Even though the participants differed in status, age, and experience, there remained a sense of oneness among them through _pakikipagkapwa_. The _kuwentuhan_ becomes a bridge through which one enters the life of another, making mutual understanding visible.
- One teenage respondent shared a heartwarming account of reconnecting with a former high school classmate during a jeepney ride home from España to Manila. What began as a long ride filled with conversation eventually became the start of their romantic relationship. The respondent could hardly believe that it was inside a jeepney where he would find his former classmate again and where their relationship would take shape. Starting from the front section of the jeepney near the driver, the classmate moved to sit beside him, and they began to greet each other and catch up. Their conversation deepened into what they were currently doing and their activities in school. Their relationship lasted nearly a year and a half.
- Another respondent shared that pets also became the subject of conversation. Passengers exchanged information about affordable places to buy pet food and supplies, basic home remedies when their pets were sick, and their personal experiences in caring for animals. Brief as the conversation was, it became genuinely interesting to the other passengers, as they could clearly see the enthusiasm and connection shared among those involved through the common experience of pet ownership.
- The act of _kuwentuhan_ inside the jeepney applies equally to _ibang-tao_ and _hindi ibang-tao_. It becomes an inclusive activity for everyone, depending on one's willingness to be part of the conversation. Experienced within it are levels of social interaction such as _pakikiisa_, _pakikisangkot_, and _pakikipagpalagayang-loob_. Small as the space and brief as the time may be in the respondents' encounters, what they carried with them was an indication that deepened their understanding of _kapwa_ as experienced among fellow passengers.

# +Limos at Malasakit
## Cabilin et al., 2025
- The teenage respondents shared that _pakikipagkapwa_ manifests even in small ways. One respondent found that giving alms inside the jeepney had a deeper impact on him compared to giving to beggars on the street. He developed a more profound appreciation for the value of extending help, having witnessed _pakikipagkapwa_ being practiced by his fellow passengers as well.
- The physical closeness among passengers is also a product of the jeepney's structure — its narrow space and face-to-face seating deepen each person's awareness of the other, fostering _pakikipagkapwa_. The act of helping one another thus becomes contagious inside the jeepney, rooted in the distinctly Filipino values of _hiya_ and _pakikisama_.
- Filipinos believe they must act in accordance with what society considers acceptable behavior. This is where the feeling of _hiya_ emerges — as one respondent shared, he felt ashamed to remain indifferent when alms were passed directly in front of him or handed through him, while others around him were giving. From _hiya_ grows the disposition of _pakikisama_ within him, as he desires to maintain good and harmonious relations with others. Because others gave, he felt compelled to join in and pass the coins along. It can be said that a genuine _bayanihan_ takes place inside the jeepney — not only in the passing of fares from the back of the vehicle to the driver, but also in the giving of alms, as passengers come together to help those in need. He no longer becomes _ibang-tao_ inside the jeepney but _kapwa-tao_ — one who joins and moves together with fellow passengers.
- He becomes a fellow passenger who is no longer _ibang-tao_, as one can already see in him the kindness, generosity, and readiness to help others that are innate to Filipinos. Aside from joining in the passing of fares, the respondent also experienced occasions where fellow passengers asked him how much the correct fare was to a certain place, whether the jeepney passed by their destination, or engaged him in brief conversations and exchanges of opinion.
- He holds an equal regard for his fellow passengers, seeing them all as ordinary commuters despite their differences in social standing. For him, whenever he is able to talk with a fellow passenger, he feels a sense of normalcy — of being a regular person — as he gradually builds trust in others through the _pakikipagpalagayang-loob_ he develops with fellow passengers inside the jeepney.
- Even in the seating arrangements inside the jeepney, the teenage respondents observed a culture of giving without expecting anything in return. This is proof that _pakikipagkapwa_ comes naturally to Filipinos even without being told to practice it. Even in the simple act of a passenger, _pakikipagkapwa_ can be seen through the act of giving — even in something as ordinary as making room for others in the jeepney. It is evident from the experiences shared that through _pakiramdaman_ toward fellow passengers, there arises a need to give, and that giving to others is a clear indication of _pakikipagkapwa_.

# +Caring for the Elderly (Parrucho, 2023)
- The first sub-theme focuses on caring for the elderly as a Filipino value demonstrated during a Jeepney ride. From the gathered data, the care for the elderly is performed through assistance by younger commuters and by giving up seats for those in need.
- the primary Filipino value that was evident in jeepney rides was care for the elderly. According to Oropilla and Guadana (2021), the elderly or "senior citizens" are treated with the utmost respect in Philippine culture. Senior citizens and elders have the closest seats to the entrance, allowing them to easily sit and go. The DOTR issued Department Order No. 2014- 013 directing senior citizens, pregnant women, and people with disabilities to be given priority seating in public transportation, particularly jeepneys and buses. Younger passengers assist the elderly in boarding or bear heavy loads. This suggests that Filipinos place a high cultural value on age and the elderly (Teng et al., 2021).

# -Pagwawalang Bahala (Cabilin, 2025)
- The tendency toward indifference comes naturally to some Filipinos, rooted in a disregard for others, and this is clearly observable in several scenes inside the jeepney according to the teenage respondents. Indifference inside the jeepney, according to the respondents, knows no age. The most common manifestation is the frequent act of deliberately ignoring a fellow passenger who is asking for help in passing their fare to the driver, particularly when seated far from the front. This occurs when fellow passengers are indifferent or simply too lazy to pass the payment along. There are also those who do not make room for others, and those who pay no attention to passengers asking for alms. They turn their gaze elsewhere or busy themselves with something else, such as using their phones.
- Another situation reflecting indifference involves drivers who speed recklessly, prompting some passengers to speak up without hesitation and remind the driver to slow down. One respondent even shared a personal experience where the driver started moving before a passenger had fully alighted, causing that passenger to fall.

# -Pagkagalit at Pagdadabog (Cabilin, 2025)
- Some passengers become visibly angry when their signal to stop is not acted upon immediately, especially when the jeepney has already passed their intended drop-off point. Drivers sometimes retaliate against short-tempered passengers by dropping them off in an inconvenient location, or simply stopping wherever the signal reached them without properly pulling to the side. 
- The potential accident this poses for their passengers is of no concern to them. The driver's irritation or annoyance can also be read from his expression reflected in the mirror when a passenger is slow to alight. On the other hand, when the driver does not hear the _pára_ signal right away, the passenger responds by knocking aggressively on the roof and repeating _pára_ loudly. These situations are frequently observed among drivers and passengers with short tempers, resulting in visible frustration and outbursts on both sides.
# -Tsismisan (Cabilin, 2025)
- Passengers often bring the stories of their daily lives into the jeepney. Based on one respondent's personal experience, he and his classmate once gossiped about another classmate inside the jeepney, not realizing that the person they were talking about was seated right at the front near the driver.
- Adding to the gossip is the loud volume of conversation that disrupts the silence or the brief rest of other passengers during the ride. The driver's short exchanges with fellow drivers when they pull up alongside each other in traffic — about scarce passengers, enforcers on patrol, traffic conditions, and passengers asking for change — also prove disruptive to those riding inside.

# -Siksikan at Singitan (Cabilin, 2025)
- It is a common scene at the jeepney terminal for the barker to urge passengers to move closer together, forcing what is meant to be seating for eight to become nine or ten — especially if the passengers on the edges are on the slimmer side.
- For drivers, this means additional income, especially when they are queued up and paying their _butaw_, even if it means their passengers are packed together like sardines in a can. This is a normal sight inside the jeepney — passengers crammed tightly on the seats. Because of this, some passengers will lean slightly outward to make proper room for the person seated beside them.
- Discounts are unheard of inside the jeepney even when only half the seats are filled. When you are in a hurry, you no longer care whether you are seated comfortably or not. There are also many who take advantage of the situation inside the jeepney — exploiting the tight and pressing circumstances that passengers find themselves in.
- In the cramped and competitive scramble for seats, the stronger passengers take advantage of the weaker ones. Those with larger and stronger bodies, typically men, are quick to secure their seats.
- Senior citizens, persons with disabilities, pregnant women, parents carrying infants or minding young children, and students who do not know how to push their way through are most often left at a disadvantage.

# -Budol (Cabilin, 2025)
- Children who beg also board the jeepney, carrying shoe-cleaning materials, then ask for alms in exchange for a brief and perfunctory pretense of wiping passengers' shoes.
- There is also the young woman dressed in white who suddenly squeezes into a seat, carrying a Bible, reads a verse, shares a word of God, and passes around small envelopes for offerings.
- Then there is the young man with a _Maalala Mo Kaya_-worthy story about a family member in the hospital, showing a medical bill as he asks for alms.
- According to the teenage respondents, these are, upon reflection, modus operandi that are difficult to take at face value — having witnessed these schemes repeatedly, they have come to recognize them as forms of taking advantage of fellow passengers. These represent various types of deception and manipulation that erode the willingness to help others, as they breed doubt over whether the accounts of those asking for alms are genuine or not.

# -Lamangan (Cabilin, 2025)
- Alongside the culture of giving, the practice of _lamangan_ is also noticeably present inside the jeepney — such as the so-called "123," which refers to bolting off without paying the fare.
- There are passengers who pretend to hand over their payment but come up short, then quickly alight from the jeepney before anyone can address it.
- The _lamangan_ inside the jeepney becomes an opportunity to develop a critical awareness of the varied experiences that reflect the realities of life in Filipino society. One respondent shared that it is ultimately up to each of them how they process these experiences within themselves. Others commented that what matters most is that one continues to choose to be a good person toward fellow passengers in every situation.

# -Pakutuban (Cabilin, 2025)
- The respondents mentioned that a certain mix of anxiety builds up inside them, making it impossible to avoid being watchful of their surroundings — particularly at night when the streets are sparse and they are riding the jeepney.
- This stems from what they see in the news or from scenes depicted in films, such as holdups. The teenage respondents clarified that this is not a constant state of fear, but that it is better to remain vigilant and always prepared for unexpected events of this kind. They further noted that in today's times, it is difficult to judge who among the people sharing the jeepney may have ill intentions — as reports show that children, the elderly, women, and men alike have all fallen victim to such incidents inside the jeepney.
